MS Lab: Isotope Correction Tool

The Isotope Correction Tool is an in-house software package that corrects raw abundances for the natural abundance of heavy isotopes of elements.
As an output an excel file with four tabs is created (RawAbundance, CorrectedIsotopologues, FractionalContribution and RawIntensity).

For now the buttons below are not yet functional, this feature will be added further in the educational program.

In the meantime an email containing the raw data can be sent to the developer. When the calculations are finished, a reply will be sent to you with the corrected file.

How it works:

The mass spectrometer measures compounds as an electric signal, this signal is visualized as a peak in a spectrum. The software detects these peaks and measure their height and assigns a molecular formula to each one depending on the mass off the peak.

Each element is this molecular formula will have a stable isotope that also exists in nature. Since we are not looking at nature but at our experimental conditions, we want to correct the peak height for the natural abundance of each element..

As a result we get an excel file with four tabs: Raw Abundance, Corrected isotopologues, fractional contribution and raw intensity.

Raw Abundance

In the world of mass spectrometry, raw abundance serves as the foundational data point for isotopic analysis. As molecules are ionized and subjected to mass spectrometric analysis, the resulting signals are converted into peaks on a mass spectrum. These peaks represent different isotopic variants of the same molecule. For example, carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen isotopes can vary, leading to different peaks for each isotopic combination. Raw abundance reflects the intensity of these peaks, providing information about the presence and relative quantities of isotopes. Researchers use raw abundance to calculate isotopic ratios, which offer insights into molecular origins, metabolic pathways, and structural information.

Corrected Isotopologues

While raw abundance is the initial measurement, the real challenge lies in extracting accurate information from noisy data. This is where corrected isotopologues come into play. Instrumental noise, background interference, and overlapping signals from other molecules can distort the raw abundance data. Corrected isotopologues involve sophisticated data processing techniques that remove noise and enhance the precision of the measurement. By refining the data, researchers gain a clearer understanding of the isotopic distribution, allowing them to distinguish genuine signals from artifacts. Corrected isotopologues enable researchers to confidently identify specific isotopic patterns and make informed decisions about metabolite origins and pathways.

Fractional Contribution

Fractional contribution takes isotopic analysis a step further by providing insights into the relative proportions of isotopes within a molecule. For instance, when studying a metabolite, understanding how much of the molecule contains each isotopic variant is crucial. This information can shed light on metabolic reactions, biosynthetic pathways, and even sources of substrates. Fractional contribution analysis helps researchers discern whether a specific isotope is preferentially incorporated into a molecule during biosynthesis or if certain metabolic processes influence isotopic ratios. By quantifying the fractional contribution of each isotopic variant, scientists can paint a detailed picture of how molecules are formed, modified, and utilized within biological systems.

Raw Intensity

In the realm of mass spectrometry, raw intensity serves as a fundamental measurement that unveils the intricacies of molecules and their isotopic compositions. This critical concept contributes to the understanding of molecular structures, metabolic pathways, and the identification of compounds within complex biological samples.
